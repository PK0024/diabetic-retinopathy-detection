About Diabetic Retinopathy

What is Diabetic Retinopathy?

Diabetic Retinopathy (DR) is a diabetes complication that affects the eyes. It's caused by damage to the blood vessels of the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye (retina).

At first, diabetic retinopathy may cause no symptoms or only mild vision problems. Eventually, it can cause blindness. The condition can develop in anyone who has type 1 or type 2 diabetes. The longer you have diabetes and the less controlled your blood sugar is, the more likely you are to develop this eye complication.

Severity Levels

Level 0: No Diabetic Retinopathy

No signs of diabetic retinopathy. The retina appears normal.

Level 1: Mild Non-proliferative DR

Early stage with microaneurysms (small areas of balloon-like swelling in the retina's tiny blood vessels).

Level 2: Moderate Non-proliferative DR

Blood vessels that nourish the retina are blocked. More microaneurysms and some hemorrhages.

Level 3: Severe Non-proliferative DR

More blood vessels are blocked, depriving several areas of the retina of their blood supply.

Level 4: Proliferative DR

Most advanced stage. New, abnormal blood vessels grow in the retina, which can lead to serious vision problems.

Symptoms

  • Spots or dark strings floating in your vision (floaters)
  • Blurred vision
  • Fluctuating vision
  • Impaired color vision
  • Dark or empty areas in your vision
  • Vision loss

Prevention

  • Manage your diabetes by keeping blood sugar levels in target range
  • Control high blood pressure and cholesterol
  • Quit smoking if you smoke
  • Pay attention to vision changes
  • Have regular eye exams

Treatment

Treatment depends on the type and severity of diabetic retinopathy. Early stages may only require monitoring. Advanced stages may require laser treatment, injections, or surgery. Early detection and treatment can help prevent vision loss.
